You Don't Own Your Website

Why living entirely inside Squarespace, Wix, or Shopify makes you a tenant — not an owner.

7 min read · June 2026

Here's a question most business owners have never actually asked themselves: if your website platform disappeared tomorrow, doubled its prices overnight, or suspended your account — what would you actually be left with?

For a lot of small businesses and B-Corps, the honest answer is: nothing. No copy of your content in a usable format. No control over your own domain routing. No way to simply "move" your site somewhere else, because there was never really a site to move — just a rented space inside someone else's system, dressed up to look like your own.

This is platform capture, and it's worth understanding clearly, because it's rarely explained to the people it affects most.

What "platform capture" actually means

When you build your business on Squarespace, Wix, Shopify, or similar all-in-one platforms, you're not really building a website. You're building inside someone else's product, using their tools, their infrastructure, and their rules.

That distinction sounds abstract until you look at what it actually means in practice:

  • Your content lives in their format. Export tools are often limited, and what comes out rarely resembles what you built — layouts break, custom styling disappears, and structured content becomes a mess of plain text.
  • Your site's performance depends on their infrastructure. You didn't choose the servers, the caching strategy, or the code quality running underneath your pages. If it's slow, bloated, or has accessibility issues, that's largely out of your hands.
  • Your pricing is their decision. Many of these platforms have quietly raised prices, restructured tiers, or pushed users toward more expensive plans over time. As a tenant, you don't get a vote.
  • Your business depends on their continued existence. Platforms get acquired, sunset, or deprioritized. It's happened before, and businesses built entirely on top of them had no real recourse.

None of this is a conspiracy. It's simply how the business model works: the platform's job is to keep you inside it, paying monthly, for as long as possible. That's not a criticism of the people who work there — it's just the incentive structure of renting.

"But it looks like my website"

This is the part that catches people off guard. A well-designed Squarespace or Wix site can look completely polished, professional, and bespoke. The visual layer is genuinely yours — the colors, the photos, the words you wrote.

But underneath that layer, none of the actual infrastructure belongs to you. You don't own the code. You can't take your site's backend and move it to a different host. You can't hand it to a different developer and say "here, pick up where this left off," because there's no codebase to hand over — just a locked configuration inside a platform you're renting access to.

It's the digital equivalent of decorating a rented apartment beautifully and then realizing, only when you try to move, that you never actually owned the walls.

Why this matters more for mission-driven businesses

If you're a B-Corp or a values-driven SME, platform capture carries an extra layer of tension. A lot of these all-in-one platforms are US-based companies with data practices, hosting locations, and terms of service that may not reflect the standards you've built your own business around — GDPR compliance, data minimization, transparency about where customer information actually lives.

You can build the most ethical, thoughtful brand in the world, and still have your customers' data sitting on infrastructure you have no visibility into, governed by a privacy policy you didn't write and can't negotiate. That's a real gap between what a business says it stands for and what it's actually built on.

What ownership actually looks like

None of this means every small business needs a custom-built, fully bespoke web application from day one — that would be overkill for plenty of businesses, and there's nothing wrong with using a page builder to get started. The problem isn't using tools. The problem is not knowing the difference between using a tool and being trapped inside one.

Real ownership looks like:

  • A codebase you actually hold, even if someone else built it for you — one that can be handed to any competent developer, not just the original platform's ecosystem.
  • Hosting you choose, with visibility into where your data physically lives and who has access to it.
  • A domain and infrastructure that are portable — if you want to leave a host, an email provider, or a developer, you can, without losing your site, your data, or your history.
  • No mystery dependencies — no situation where a single company's pricing decision or shutdown notice could take your business offline.

This doesn't have to be complicated or expensive. It's a different starting posture: build (or ask your developer to build) in a way where you always retain the keys, rather than being handed a beautifully decorated room you can never actually own.

The real question to ask

Not "does my website look good" — plenty of platform-built sites do. The question is: if I needed to leave tomorrow, could I?

If the honest answer is no, that's not a reason to panic or rebuild everything overnight. It's just worth knowing clearly, so it's a choice you're making on purpose — not a dependency you backed into without realizing it.
