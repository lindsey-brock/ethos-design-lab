What Your Web Agency Isn't Telling You

The hidden costs, lock-in, and quiet trade-offs behind "easy" website solutions.

8 min read · July 2026

In the first post in this series, we talked about platform capture — what happens when your website lives entirely inside Squarespace, Wix, or Shopify, and you end up a tenant rather than an owner.

But platform capture isn't only a DIY problem. It happens just as often — sometimes more quietly — when you do hire a professional. Most business owners assume that paying an agency automatically means they're getting ownership, quality, and independence in return. Often, they're not. They're getting a polished version of the same trap, with a bigger invoice attached.

Here's what tends to go unsaid in that first sales call.

"Custom" doesn't always mean what you think it means

Here's a distinction that rarely gets explained clearly: there's a real difference between an agency building you a fully custom site and an agency building you a site inside a platform — Framer, Webflow, WordPress with a premium theme and page builder — and calling it custom because the design is bespoke.

The visual result can look identical. The underlying reality is not.

If your site is built inside Framer or Webflow, there's often an ongoing platform subscription required just to keep the site live — separate from whatever you're paying the agency. WordPress is technically open-source and free, but in practice a professional build usually depends on a stack of premium plugins and themes (page builders like Elementor or Divi, security plugins, SEO plugins, form plugins), many of which carry their own annual licensing fees. Miss a renewal on the wrong plugin, and parts of your site can quietly break.

None of this makes the agency dishonest — plenty of good, complex work gets done on these platforms, and building on WordPress or Framer is a completely reasonable choice for lots of businesses. But it does mean you've inherited a second layer of ongoing cost and dependency that a fully hand-coded site wouldn't have, and it's often never spelled out as a distinction at all. "We build custom websites" can be technically true while still meaning "custom design, on top of a platform you'll be renting indefinitely."

Worth asking plainly: is this site custom-coded, or built on top of a platform — and if it's the latter, what are the ongoing subscription or licensing costs I should expect, beyond your fee?

"We'll build it for you" doesn't always mean "you'll own it"

This is the single most important question most business owners never ask: when the project is finished, what exactly do I have?

A surprising number of agencies build client sites on their own agency account inside a platform like Squarespace or Webflow — not because it's technically necessary, but because it's convenient for them. It means:

  • They control the login.
  • They control the billing.
  • If you ever want to leave, you're not migrating a website — you're negotiating for access to your own business.

The "maintenance plan" that's really just life support

Almost every agency offers some version of an ongoing maintenance retainer. Some of these are genuinely valuable — active updates, new features, real strategic input. But a lot of them quietly amount to this:

We will keep the thing we built for you from breaking.

That's not nothing, but it's also not much. If a "maintenance" fee is mostly covering plugin updates, basic uptime monitoring, and the agency's own hosting markup, you're paying monthly rent on top of the original build cost — for a site that isn't getting meaningfully better, just kept from actively failing.

Worth asking directly: what does this retainer actually include, month to month, in concrete terms? If the answer is vague, that's information too.

Hosting markup: the fee inside the fee

Many agencies resell hosting — sometimes their own infrastructure, sometimes a wrapped version of a third-party provider — at a significant markup over what it would cost you directly. This isn't inherently unfair; there's real work in managing hosting, backups, and uptime. But it's rarely disclosed as a line item. It gets folded into "maintenance" or "care plan" language, so you never see the actual server cost versus what you're being charged for access to it.

A reasonable question: if I wanted to see exactly what the underlying hosting costs, separate from your management fee, could you show me? A confident answer is a good sign. Evasiveness is worth noting.

SEO that was never built to last

"SEO included" is one of the most common phrases in agency proposals, and one of the least consistently defined. Sometimes it means a thoughtful technical foundation — proper site structure, fast load times, clean metadata, structured data. Sometimes it means a handful of keyword-stuffed blog posts published once, with no ongoing strategy behind them.

The tell is usually in what happens after launch. If SEO was treated as a checkbox during the build rather than an ongoing practice, rankings tend to plateau or quietly decline within a year — and by then, the agency relationship has often moved on to the next client.

The real cost of "free" tools

This applies beyond agencies, but it's worth including here because agencies often recommend these tools as part of a build: many "free" platforms (email marketing tools, form builders, analytics dashboards, live chat widgets) generate their revenue from your data, not your subscription. You're not the customer in the traditional sense — you're the product, or your visitors are.

That's not automatically disqualifying. But it's a trade-off you should be making consciously, not one that gets bundled invisibly into a proposal because it was the easiest thing for the agency to plug in.

What to actually ask before you sign

A short, direct list, worth bringing to any first conversation with a prospective agency:

  • Is this site fully custom-coded, or built on a platform — and what are the ongoing subscription/licensing costs beyond your fee?
  • Who owns the final codebase, domain, and hosting account — by default, in writing?
  • What happens, step by step, if I want to leave in two years?
  • What exactly is included in the ongoing retainer, in concrete deliverables, not vague language?
  • Is hosting itemized separately from management fees, and can I see the difference?
  • Where does my (and my customers') data actually live, and under whose privacy policy?

None of these questions are hostile. A good agency will answer them clearly and without defensiveness — because a good agency isn't relying on you not asking.

The pattern underneath all of this

Platform capture and agency lock-in aren't really two separate problems. They're the same problem wearing different clothes: a system that works best for you also happens to be a system you can leave at any time, on your own terms, without losing anything in the process.

That's a genuinely reasonable thing to ask for. It's also, quietly, not the default in most of this industry — which is exactly why it's worth asking about explicitly, rather than assuming it's already true.
