What Independent Web Infrastructure Actually Looks Like

The alternative to platform capture and agency lock-in — in practical terms.

7 min read · August 2026

The first two posts in this series covered the problem from two angles: what happens when your business lives entirely inside a platform like Squarespace or Wix (part one), and what happens when an agency builds you something that looks custom but really isn't (part two).

This last post asks: what does the actual alternative look like?

This matters because "independent," "custom," and "you own it" get used loosely enough in this industry that they've started to mean almost nothing on their own. So instead of adjectives, here's a list of concrete facts you can verify.

1. You can name your hosting provider

Not "my agency handles that" — an actual provider, on an account with your name and your billing details attached. It might be Vercel, Netlify, a VPS, or a managed host — the specific choice matters less than the fact that you know what it is and could access it directly if you needed to.

If you can't answer "who hosts my website" without asking someone else first, that's not a small technical detail. It's the whole ballgame.

2. Your codebase exists somewhere you control

A real, versioned codebase — typically on GitHub or similar — that you have access to, ideally as the account owner, not a guest collaborator on someone else's account. This is what actually gets handed over at the end of a project when infrastructure is done right: not a login to a page builder, but a repository.

This is also what makes a developer relationship replaceable without being catastrophic. If your current developer becomes unavailable, retires, or you simply want to work with someone else, a new developer can open that repository and understand what they're working with. Without it, a new developer is starting from a screenshot of your old site and rebuilding from scratch — which is functionally the same as never having owned anything in the first place.

3. Your domain is registered in your name, at a registrar you control

Domains sometimes get registered by an agency "to make things easier" and never get transferred to the client afterward. Years later, that agency is the only entity that can technically move your domain, renew it, or point it somewhere else. Check the WHOIS record. If the registrant isn't you or your business, that's worth resolving regardless of how good the current relationship is.

4. There's no subscription standing between you and your own site being online

If your website stops working the moment a third-party platform subscription lapses — not your hosting bill, but a page-builder license, a premium plugin renewal, a website builder's monthly plan — that's a dependency worth knowing about explicitly, even if you're comfortable with it. The goal isn't necessarily zero dependencies; very few real systems have none. The goal is knowing exactly what they are, rather than discovering them the day something breaks.

5. Your data has a knowable location and a knowable policy

For a business that takes data seriously — GDPR compliance, transparency with customers, basic operational hygiene — "I don't actually know where our data lives" shouldn't be an acceptable answer. Independent infrastructure means being able to say specifically: this is where customer data is stored, this is the privacy policy governing it, and this is who has access.

6. Leaving is a plan, not a crisis

The clearest test of all: if you wrote down, right now, the steps required to move your website to a new host, a new developer, or a new agency entirely — could you actually write that list? Does it involve exporting a repository and updating DNS, or does it involve a series of increasingly tense emails asking someone else to please hand things over?

If it's the former, you're in a genuinely independent setup. If it's the latter, you're not — regardless of how the original sales conversation was framed.

What this is actually for

None of this is a purity test, and it's not a reason to distrust every agency or rebuild a perfectly functional website out of anxiety. Plenty of businesses are well-served by simpler, platform-based setups, especially early on, when speed and cost matter more than long-term independence.

The point of this series isn't "platforms bad, custom code good" as a blanket rule. It's narrower and more useful than that: you should know which one you have, on purpose, rather than discovering it under pressure — when a renewal is missed, when a relationship ends badly, or when the business has grown past what the original setup was built for.

That's the actual dividing line. Not whether your site was built on a platform or from scratch, but whether you were ever given an honest, plain-language answer to the six questions above — before you needed one.

This closes out our short series on digital ownership for small businesses. If you're not sure how your own setup holds up against this list, that's a completely reasonable thing to want a second opinion on.
